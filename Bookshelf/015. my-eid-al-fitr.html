Focus Question

What is Eid al-Fitr, and how does the girl's family celebrate it?

Words to Know

  • agreed
  • Eid al-Fitr
  • excited
  • Muslims
  • new moon
  • Ramadan

Ramadan (RAH-mah-dahn) was almost over.

I was so excited.

Soon it would be time for Eid al-Fitr (EED ul-FIT-ur).

After the Sun went down, I ate dinner with my family.

Then the family who just moved in next door came over for tea.

Their son was nine, like me.

His name was Shadi.

Shadi and his family are Muslims, and so is my family.

Shadi's family just moved here from another country.

It was not safe there.

We talked about how they had to leave their home.

I thought about how I felt three years ago.

My family also had to leave our home country.

Later, I looked for the new moon with Shadi.

A new moon meant Ramadan was over and Eid al-Fitr was here.

The next day, I saw family and friends.

They gave me money and gifts for Eid.

Many children I know get presents for Eid, like me.

Since Shadi had just moved, I knew he would not get much this year.

I went to see Shadi right away.

I gave him one of my gifts.

I told Shadi that I also got money to spend at the Eid fair.

I told him all about the fair.

It's my favorite part of Eid.

I asked Shadi to come with me to the fair.

My new friend agreed.

This was the best Eid yet!

Ramadan and Eid al-Fitr

Ramadan is a special month for Muslim people.

During Ramadan, Muslims do not eat or drink during the day.

Eid al-Fitr is a holiday that celebrates the end of Ramadan.

Words to Know

Eid al-Fitr (n.)
a three-day holiday that many Muslims celebrate at the end of Ramadan by praying, eating, and giving gifts

excited (adj.)
very eager and interested

Muslims (n.)
people who follow the religion of Islam

new moon (n.)
the first phase of the Moon, when its dark side is toward Earth or when it is very narrow afterward

Ramadan (n.)
a month-long holiday that many Muslims celebrate by not eating between sunrise and sunset
